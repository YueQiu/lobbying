Lobbying in the United States describes paid activity in which special interests hire well-connected professional advocates, often lawyers, to argue for specific legislation in decision-making bodies such as the United States Congress.

Lobbying expenditures for large pro-business lobbying groups soared in 2012, driven by election-year politics, the lame-duck congressional session's increased legislation, and the fiscal cliff showdown,

A study of the 50 firms that spent the most on lobbying relative to their assets compared their financial performance against that of the S&P 500 in the stock market concluded that spending on lobbying was a "spectacular investment" yielding "blistering" returns comparable to a high-flying hedge fund, even despite the financial downturn of the past few years.

Lobbying and Industry Performance (1998-2012)

Explore the "Year" navigator to visualize trends over time, or filter by sector.

 

Formally, lobbying intends to influence the passage or blockage of legislative bills and regulations. However, given that many firms, organizations, and people often lobby the same piece of legislation but on opposing sides, a better way of measuring the "success" of lobbying is by examining the relationship between the amount spent on lobbying by a firm or industry, and that industry's overall performance.

Overall, from 1998 to 2012, there has been a strong, positive relationship between and industry's lobbying efforts and the gross output of that industry. In other words, industries that invest a lot of funds on lobbying tend to, on average, have higher gross output. However, this does not necessarily represent a causal relationship.

Indeed, there is wide industry-to-industry variation, particularly on a year-to-year basis, that suggest otherwise. Here are some interesting facts.

 

AGRIBUSINESS

Crop production and basic processing has grown very rapidly in terms of gross output while lobbying has grown much more slowly.
The tobacco industry's lobbying efforts have varied widely from year-to-year, with little effect on the performance of the industry

CONSTRUCTION

The housing boom and bust are evident: the home builder industry's gross output increases dramatically up to 2006 then falls sharply after 2007, with little change in lobbying efforts.

ENERGY

The oil and gas industry suffered a significant drop in gross output at the beginning of the great recession despite increases in lobbying funds.
The electric utilities industry has more than doubled lobbying funds over the past 12 years, yet its performance as an industry has stayed constant

FINANCE

From 2002 to 2007, there was a positive correlation between lobbying money and gross output in the banking/finance, insurance, and real estate industries. Since 2008 (the beginning of the financial crisis), increased lobbying is correlated with lower gross output
